In otolaryngology (Ear, Nose, and Throat medicine), upper respiratory, head, and neck infections are classified by their precise anatomical location. Because these anatomical compartments are connected by fascial planes, minor infections can sometimes travel into deep spaces. [1, 2, 3]

The comprehensive list of infections occurring across these regions is categorized below by exact location.

1\. Sinus and Nasal Cavity Infections
-------------------------------------

-   Acute Rhinosinusitis: Short-term inflammation of the nasal passages and paranasal cavities, usually viral (rhinoviruses) or bacterial (*Streptococcus pneumoniae*). [4, 5, 6, 7, 8]
-   Chronic Rhinosinusitis (CRS): Prolonged sinus inflammation lasting over 12 weeks, often complicated by bacterial biofilms or nasal polyps. [2, 9]
-   Invasive Fungal Sinusitis: A highly destructive fungal infection (e.g., *Mucor* or *Aspergillus*) that can invade tissue and bone, primarily affecting immunocompromised individuals. [2, 10, 11, 12]
-   Fungal Ball (Mycetoma): A non-invasive clump of dense fungal debris that populates a single sinus cavity, usually the maxillary sinus. [4, 13, 14, 15, 16]
-   Vestibulitis: A localized bacterial infection of the nasal vestibule, typically at the skin-mucosa junction, often caused by *Staphylococcus aureus*. [17, 18]
-   Nasal Septal Abscess: A collection of pus between the septal cartilage and its perichondrium, usually secondary to trauma or untreated vestibulitis.

2\. Throat and Laryngeal Infections
-----------------------------------

-   Acute Pharyngitis: Inflammation of the pharynx (sore throat), commonly caused by viruses (adenovirus, Epstein-Barr) or Group A *Streptococcus*.
-   Acute Tonsillitis: Infection concentrated on the palatine tonsils, leading to severe swelling and exudate.
-   Laryngitis: Infection and swelling of the larynx (voice box), leading to hoarseness or voice loss.
-   Epiglottitis: A rapid, life-threatening bacterial infection of the epiglottis that can completely block the airway.
-   Laryngotracheobronchitis (Croup): A viral upper airway infection common in children that causes a characteristic "barking" cough.
-   Supraglottitis: Infection of the structures located right above the vocal cords. [2, 3, 19, 20, 21]

3\. Head, Cranial, and Facial Infections
----------------------------------------

-   Otitis Externa & Media: Infection of the outer ear canal ("swimmer's ear") or middle ear space behind the eardrum.
-   Malignant Otitis Externa: A severe, necrotizing infection of the external auditory canal that spreads to the skull base, commonly seen in elderly patients with diabetes.
-   Mastoiditis: A bacterial infection that spreads from the middle ear into the porous mastoid bone of the skull.
-   Sialadenitis & Parotitis: Bacterial or viral infection (e.g., mumps) of the salivary glands, such as the parotid or submandibular glands.
-   Odontogenic Infections: Dental and periodontal abscesses originating in the teeth or gums that can rapidly breach the jaw bone.
-   Orbital Cellulitis: Infection of the soft tissues within the eye socket, which frequently spreads outward from infected ethmoid sinuses.
-   Intracranial Abscesses: Brain abscesses, subdural empyemas, or epidural abscesses triggered when sinus or ear infections track upward through bone or blood vessels.
-   Cavernous Sinus Thrombosis: A rare, septic blood clot inside the brain's cavernous sinus, caused by the spread of bacteria from a facial, nasal, or sinus infection. [2, 9, 19, 22, 23]

4\. Neck and Deep Fascial Space Infections
------------------------------------------

Deep neck space infections (DNSIs) occur inside the hidden potential spaces created by the complex layers of the cervical fascia. These require urgent surgical or medical evaluation: [1, 3, 20, 24]

-   Peritonsillar Abscess (Quinsy): A localized collection of pus in the potential space between the tonsil capsule and the surrounding pharyngeal muscles.
-   Parapharyngeal Abscess: An infection in the space lateral to the pharynx, often spreading from the tonsils or teeth, which can compress the carotid artery or airway.
-   Retropharyngeal Abscess: An infection behind the pharynx, located anterior to the prevertebral fascia, frequently arising from infected lymph nodes in children.
-   Ludwig's Angina: A rapidly spreading, bilateral cellulitis of the submandibular, sublingual, and submental spaces, typically originating from an infected lower molar and causing dangerous tongue elevation.
-   Danger Space & Prevertebral Space Infections: Severe infections located directly in front of the spine that can travel continuously down into the chest cavity (mediastinum).
-   Cervical Lymphadenitis: Bacterial, viral, or mycobacterial (e.g., scrofula/tuberculosis) infections that cause painful swelling within the lymph nodes of the neck.
-   Cervical Necrotizing Fasciitis: A highly destructive, rapidly progressing bacterial infection that destroys the skin, fat, and fascial tissue layers of the neck.
-   Lemierre's Syndrome: A severe complication where a throat or tonsil infection spreads to cause an infected blood clot (septic thrombophlebitis) in the internal jugular vein. [1, 2, 19, 20, 24, 25]
